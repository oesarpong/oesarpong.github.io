Lab 8: Stunts

Introduction

In this lab, I aimed to combine all of the learned experience from the other labs by performing a fast stunt.

Task A: Flip

For this stunt, the robot will start at a designated line, drive forward as fast as possible, before reaching a wall in which it will perform a flip, and return back in the direction of where it came from.

Programming Algorithim/Logic

To accomplish this flip, I divided the problem into two steps: forward and reverse. The robot would run to the setpoint and once this setpoint would actuate the wheels to spin in the reverse at full speed. This is a callable command, FLIP(), which is shown below.

Adjusting Weight Distribution

On of the key factors to making the RC Car flip is going to be the center of mass (COM). By adding additional weight to front of the car, I can shift the COM toward the front axis, giving a larger torque about those wheels aiding in the flipping motion of the car. To do this, I used a lot of nuts and duck tape. Below is the before and after.

Discussion

Overall, I believe that the solution I came up with was acceptable. I found the flip task to be easier then the drift task due to simplified trouble shooting, seeing as I did not need to adjust any controller but included more open loop commands. In my case, one of the most pivotal features affecting performance was the amount of dirt build up on the wheels. The successful attempts shown above were after whiping the wheels off to reduce the amount of slipping caused. Therefore, although my results were repeatable there was a signficiant amount of environmental control/intervention that was needed to develop this uniformity.

Another area of interest within these attempts is the setpoint. Based on the graphs, the RC robot was sending inputs to reverse earlier then when it actually flipped and started moving in the desired direction. The TOF sensor data reaching zero actually shows when the flip started. This can be explained by the cars overwhelming momentum which had to be counteracted by an opposing force. Therefore, to get the flip to happen closer to the wall I indulged in a significant amount of tuning in order to predict where the robot would flip.

Finally, to my surprise, I ended up not using active braking to intiate this flip seeing as the momentum at a max PWM input would still overcome the holding torque applied to the motors. Therefore in the priority of speed, the method I chose can be described as a rotation assist which aids the car in making the flip after an abrupt change in inputs that would raise the rear of the car.
